Architecture Over Art: LACMA's Demolition and Redesign

  • Author: OA Real Estate Group
  • Category: Architecture
  • Date: November 5, 2020

Art museums around the world, from New York's Metropolitan to the Louvre in Paris, have been repositories of the ingenuity and creativity of humanity. They protect artworks and provide a home that accentuates them, displaying their splendour for all to see. However, the demolition and new architectural design of the Los Angeles County Museum of Art (LACMA) is drawing backlash for undermining its critical role of highlighting creative arts, watering it down to simply being a showplace. The situation is worsened by LA citizens barely having a say in the matter. Art museums around the world, from New York's Metropolitan to the Louvre in Paris, have been repositories of the ingenuity and creativity of humanity. They protect artworks and provide a home that accentuates them, displaying their splendour for all to see. However, the demolition and new architectural design of the Los Angeles County Museum of Art (LACMA) is drawing backlash for undermining its critical role of highlighting creative arts, watering it down to simply being a showplace. The situation is worsened by LA citizens barely having a say in the matter.



The Pandemic Cover

After the stay-at-home order issued in March by LA county, the LACMA was closed to the public. While citizens made arrangements for the lockdown, demolition plans kicked into high gear. Given that the public wasn’t alerted when the process began, most only came to know about the LACMA demolition through photos and videos that popped up in their social media feeds. Many of these were snapped and recorded by residents living around the museum, who were taken by surprise as well by the changes in their neighbourhood.

Design Concerns and Public Ire

The 2013 announcement that Swiss architect Peter Zumthor would design LACMA's new campus got mixed reactions. Different issues were raised. Firstly, the architect selection was carried out without public input, with Zumthor being handpicked by Michael Govan, LACMA's director. The initial plans that were released also had to be redone since scientists hadn’t been consulted about the sensitive La Brea Tar Excavation Pits which were right in the vicinity. The new design that was produced came under harsh criticism for the reduced size of the galleries – which one would expect would be the main attraction to an art museum.


While LACMA claims that the museum will have expanded the total gallery space from the around 130,000 to 220,000 square feet, calculations published on City Watch LA show that the additions have been made for the BCAM and the Resnick Pavilion, making it a case of creative accounting. Conversely, space for the paintings, textiles, photographs and other light-sensitive art has shrunk from the 138,000 square feet in the demolished structures to a mere 27,000 square feet in the new "House" galleries in the upcoming construction. Space for hang art also reduces by 75%. Estimates vary, but they show that the new museum will lose about 10,000- to 45,000-square-feet of gallery space.

The crusade to redesign this landmark has sparked quite the ire within the art world, raising questions about the motives of the management, especially since it is a publicly funded museum. Speaking of which, let’s delve into the money troubles.

Empty Coffers and A Public Bill

The frequent revisions of the new building plans have seen donors like the Ahmanson Foundation pull out. Ahmanson has been a long-term donor, supporting LACMA for over 6 decades, with notable feats like contributing the lead donation for the launch of the museum – a whopping $2 million in 1961, to the European Old Master paintings plus sculptures worth more than $130 million donated to the museum. Despite key backers withdrawing their support, LACMA went ahead with the reconstruction project without a sufficient budget to support it.

The $117 million funding and $300 million bond approved by the 2019 county-supervisor meeting was just slightly over half of the $750 million required for the project. The COVID-19 pandemic struck and made an already-difficult financial situation worse, from the economic slowdown to the uncertainty of when normal tourist traffic numbers will resume in LA. The bond will need to be repaid, adding to the strain on the budget, putting the project in a precarious position. Dubbed the "The Incredible Shrinking Museum" by Pulitzer Prize-winning Christopher Knight, LACMA reconstruction has definitely changed the Los Angeles art scene.
